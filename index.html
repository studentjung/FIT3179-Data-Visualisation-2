Ground and Growth

Land Use vs Population Patterns in North & West Melbourne's Expanding Future

Future Land Use

Melbourne continues to experience steady population growth, with its suburbs expanding to accommodate increasing housing demand and evolving community needs. Understanding how land use is planned, and where growth is occurring, offers valuable insight into the city's development priorities, spatial distribution, and long-term sustainability. This visualisation explores the relationship between urban planning and population change across Melbourne's suburbs.

The choropleth map illustrates the percentage of land area in each suburb near the Melbourne CBD that is allocated to different future land uses. The stacked bar chart complements this by showing the absolute area (in km²) of planned land for each land use category, allowing clear comparison of how residential, infrastructure, industrial, community, and open space allocations differ across suburbs. Both charts include interactive options to select land use types or reorder suburbs by total area, enabling focused exploration of planning trends.

Tarneit exhibits one of the highest proportions of planned development, with nearly all of its area already allocated to future land uses. The Rockbank - Mount Cottrell region contains the largest absolute planned land area (≈ 40.56 km²), highlighting its role as a major growth corridor in Melbourne's west. In contrast, Wallan shows no planned industrial land use, suggesting a more residential or mixed-use development strategy.

Population

Melbourne's suburbs continue to evolve alongside the city's expansion, with population growth shaping where future development occurs. This treemap visualisation provides an overview of suburban populations in 2024, focusing on areas identified for planned land use. Each rectangle represents a suburb, grouped by its broader SA3 region, with area size proportional to total population — larger blocks indicate more residents.

The Wyndham region stands out with the highest population among the emerging development corridors, reflecting its rapid transformation and strong residential growth. Similarly, the Mickleham - Yuroke area within Tullamarine - Broadmeadows records the largest overall population among established regions, signalling a balance between mature infrastructure and continuing expansion.

Conversely, Sunbury maintains the smallest population footprint within these growth areas, highlighting its semi-rural character and limited current development. Within the Melbourne City region, the Flemington Racecourse area shows the lowest population density, largely due to land reserved for non-residential use.

Together, these population patterns reveal how urban density and development priorities vary across Melbourne's suburban landscape — offering insight into where the city's next phase of growth is most likely to occur.

Land Use vs Population

The relationship between population change and future land use offers insight into how Melbourne's urban form continues to evolve. These scatterplot visualisations compare total planned land use area against key drivers of population growth — natural increase, net internal migration, and net overseas migration — to highlight where planning activity is most responsive to demographic pressure.

Trends for natural increase and net internal migration show a clear positive correlation with total planned land use across the suburbs, while overseas migration displays a slight negative correlation, with a few notable outliers such as Rockbank - Mount Cottrell and Wallan. The majority of Melbourne's recent population growth has been driven by internal migration, particularly towards growth corridors in the west and north where large-scale housing developments are underway.

Overall population size varies widely between suburbs but shows limited correlation with planned land use area. However, population density tends to decline as the total planned land use area increases, implying that urban expansion is occurring through low-density greenfield development rather than higher-density infill. This reflects Melbourne's continued outward growth pattern, consistent with planning priorities in areas like Wyndham and Melton - Bacchus Marsh.

Summary

Melbourne continues to grow steadily, with major land use planning focused on outer suburbs like Tarneit and Rockbank - Mount Cottrell, key hubs of low-density expansion. The Wyndham region leads in population growth, driven mainly by internal migration. Overall, population density declines as planned land use increases, reflecting Melbourne's outward, greenfield-focused development pattern.
